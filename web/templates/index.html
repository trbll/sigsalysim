SIGSALY Simulator

WWII Secure Voice Communication System (1943–1946) — Educational Demonstration

SIGSALY was the world's first digital, encrypted voice system — built by Bell Labs in 1943 so Roosevelt and Churchill could talk without the Germans listening in.

Before SIGSALY, the Allies used the A-3 scrambler, which simply flipped the audio spectrum. It sounded unintelligible, but the Germans cracked it routinely using spectral analysis. SIGSALY replaced this with a fundamentally different approach: digitize speech with a vocoderA "voice coder" that decomposes speech into 10 frequency band amplitudes + pitch, sampled 50 times per second. Invented by Homer Dudley at Bell Labs in 1939., encrypt the parameters with a one-time padAn encryption method using random key values and modular arithmetic. Proved unbreakable by Claude Shannon in 1949 — the ONLY encryption with a mathematical proof of perfect secrecy., and transmit the result. The key was stored on vinyl records filled with random noise, shipped by armed courier.

This simulator lets you hear each step of that process — from the original voice, through naive scrambling (and its defeat), to the full SIGSALY system. Upload your own audio or use the built-in sample, adjust parameters, and explore.
