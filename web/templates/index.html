SIGSALY Simulator

WWII Secure Voice Communication System (1943-1946) -- Educational Demonstration

SIGSALY was the world's first digital, encrypted voice system -- built by Bell Labs in 1943 so Roosevelt and Churchill could talk without the Germans listening in.

Before SIGSALY, the Allies used the A-3 scrambler, which simply flipped the audio spectrum. It sounded unintelligible, but the Germans cracked it routinely using spectral analysisAnalyzing the frequency content of a signal. Speech has distinctive patterns (formant peaks, 1/f energy rolloff) that survive simple transformations like frequency inversion. If the pattern is still there, the signal can be recovered.. SIGSALY replaced this with a fundamentally different approach: digitize speech with a vocoderA "voice coder" that decomposes speech into 10 frequency band amplitudes + pitch, sampled 50 times per second. Invented by Homer Dudley at Bell Labs in 1939. It reduces speech to just 12 numbers per frame -- enabling encryption via arithmetic., encrypt the parameters with a one-time padAn encryption method using random key values and modular arithmetic. Proved unbreakable by Claude Shannon in 1949 -- the ONLY encryption scheme with a mathematical proof of perfect secrecy. The catch: the key must be truly random, as long as the message, and never reused., and transmit the result. The key was stored on vinyl recordsPhysical phonograph records filled with random noise from mercury-vapor vacuum tubes. Each record held 12 minutes of key material. Identical copies were shipped by armed military courier to both terminals. Used records were destroyed immediately. filled with random noise, shipped by armed courier.

This simulator lets you hear each step of that process -- from the original voice, through naive scrambling (and its defeat), to the full SIGSALY system. Upload your own audio or use the built-in sample, adjust parameters, and explore.
